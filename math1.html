Math 1 — Content & Strategy

The whole course is really three function families and the algebra that powers them. These strategies are built around that spine. Because algebra is ~⅔ of the EOC, interleave it all year — don't front-load it in one unit.

EOC weights: Algebra 36–40% · Functions 32–36% · Statistics 18–20% · Geometry 8–12%

The Anchor Move

The Rule of Four

Every function lives in four forms: table, graph, equation, context. Fluency in Math 1 is the ability to move between them. Make a four-quadrant organizer the default note-taking template for every function you meet.

ANCHORFour-quadrant template

RetentionDifferentiation

For every function family, students fill a 2×2 organizer: top-left = table, top-right = graph, bottom-left = equation, bottom-right = context/story. Quiz them by giving one quadrant and asking for the other three.

NC.M1.F-IF

Example: Context — "A gym charges a $40 joining fee plus $15/month."
Table: m = 0,1,2,3 | C = 40, 55, 70, 85
Equation: f(m) = 15m + 40
Graph: line, y-intercept 40, slope 15
Function notation check: "What does f(6) = 130 mean in words?" Moving between representations is exactly what the EOC's function-notation items reward.

Differentiation: Scaffold lane — give two quadrants, students complete the other two. Extension lane — give the context with a twist (e.g., a fee change after month 3) and ask which quadrant changes most.

Domain 1 · 36–40% of EOC

Algebra

The largest domain — and the one most easily undercut by massed practice early in the year followed by no revisitation. The strategies here assume algebra is being spiraled into Do-Nows continuously.

DERIVEFactoring — structure before technique

Retention

Before touching trinomials, students need to own the distributive property in reverse. The bridge is area models.

NC.M1.A-SSE · NC.M1.A-APR

GCF first — always: Can I pull anything out of every term? 6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3). Never skip this step; it's the most common EOC error.

Area model for trinomials: To factor x² + 7x + 12, set up a 2×2 area model. Find two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to 7 (3 and 4). Place them as the "inner" terms: (x + 3)(x + 4). Students who use the area model make far fewer sign errors than those using "guess and check."

Difference of squares: x² − 9 = (x + 3)(x − 3). The pattern recognition shortcut only works once students have factored several by hand and noticed the pattern themselves.

Error analysis pair: Show a student "solution" with a sign error in the zero-product step. "What did they do? Where did it break? Fix it." One resource, two lanes.

SEQUENCESolving quadratics — three methods, one picture

Retention

Students confuse when to use factoring vs. completing the square vs. the quadratic formula. Anchor all three to the graph — the solutions are where the parabola crosses the x-axis.

NC.M1.A-REI · NC.M1.F-IF.8

Graph first: Graph y = x² − 5x + 6. Where does it cross the x-axis? At x = 2 and x = 3. Now factor to confirm: (x − 2)(x − 3) = 0. The graph is not a shortcut — it's the source of meaning.

Method guide:
Factorable? → Factor (fastest).
Coefficient ≠ 1 or no obvious factors? → Quadratic formula (always works).
Perfect square (a = 1, b = 0)? → Square root both sides.

Quadratic formula fluency: Students must memorize x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a and be fluent substituting before the EOC. Weekly practice identifying a, b, c from a given equation builds this faster than a single unit drill.

SORTSystems of equations — when to substitute vs. eliminate

DifferentiationRetention
NC.M1.A-REI.6

Decision guide:
One equation is already solved for a variable → substitution.
Coefficients can be made equal by multiplying → elimination.
Both methods always work — efficiency is the only difference.

Context-first entry: "Two rental companies — A charges $25/day + $0.15/mile, B charges $40/day + $0.10/mile. When do they cost the same?" Students set up the system from the story before seeing any algebra.

Extension: Solve a 3×3 system for students who have mastered 2×2 and want a challenge.

Domain 2 · 32–36% of EOC

Functions

The three function families taught in contrast — not one chapter at a time — so students can discriminate between them. The EOC repeatedly asks which model fits; this section builds that skill.

CONTRASTDiscriminating between function families

Retention

Teach the families together, not sequentially in isolation. Constant first difference → linear. Constant ratio → exponential. Constant second difference → quadratic.

NC.M1.F-LE · NC.M1.F-BF

Table fingerprinting activity: Give 12 tables with no labels. Students compute differences/ratios and sort into three piles, then write the identifying fingerprint on each: "constant first diff = 3" or "constant ratio = 2."

Graph discrimination: Show three graphs (line, J-curve, U-curve). Students match to equation, then justify using the shape characteristics (constant slope → line; increasing slope → curved; symmetric vertex → parabola).

Contrast-based learning: Each family is more memorable when students learn it alongside the others. Isolating families chapter-by-chapter causes students to apply linear models to everything because that's all they know at the time.

VISUALDesmos parameter investigations

DifferentiationRetention

Students take the EOC with a built-in graphing calculator — technology fluency is a tested skill. Use Desmos sliders to let students discover how parameters move a graph before being told.

NC.M1.F-IF.7 · NC.M1.F-BF.3

Linear sliders: Graph y = mx + b with sliders. Ask: "What does changing m do? What does changing b do? What can you change to make the line go through (3, 5)?" — predict before dragging.

Exponential sliders: Graph y = a·bˣ. What happens when b > 1? When 0 < b < 1? When a doubles? Connect the slider behavior to the vocabulary: growth/decay, initial value, growth factor.

Vertex form sliders: Graph y = a(x − h)² + k. Ask students to predict the vertex's move before dragging. The "why" of vertex form lands in 10 minutes vs. weeks of algebraic manipulation.

Low floor / high ceiling: Anyone can drag a slider. The high ceiling is predicting behavior analytically before checking with the graph — that's exactly the EOC reasoning skill.

KEYExponential growth vs. decay — anchor to context

Retention
NC.M1.F-LE.1–2

General form: f(t) = a · bᵗ where a = initial value, b = growth/decay factor.
Growth: b > 1. b = 1 + r where r is the percent rate. (4% growth → b = 1.04)
Decay: 0 < b < 1. b = 1 − r. (15% depreciation → b = 0.85)

Common context bank: population growth, compound interest, radioactive decay, depreciation, viral spread. Students should be able to write f(t) from any of these and interpret a, b, and f(5) in words.

Error alert: Students frequently write b = r (the rate) instead of b = 1 + r. Have them check: "If the growth rate is 10%, what should the initial value multiply by each year? 0.10 or 1.10?"

KEYQuadratic functions — from vertex to roots

Retention
NC.M1.F-IF.7 · NC.M1.F-IF.8

Three forms and when to use each:
Standard form ax² + bx + c: easy to identify y-intercept (c), use quadratic formula for roots.
Vertex form a(x − h)² + k: vertex is (h, k), easy to graph.
Factored form a(x − r₁)(x − r₂): roots are x = r₁ and x = r₂ directly.

Entry sequence: (1) Graph the parabola. (2) Identify vertex, axis of symmetry, direction of opening, intercepts from the graph. (3) Connect each feature to a form of the equation. The algebra becomes a language for describing what they already see.

Axis of symmetry shortcut: x = −b/2a. Derive it from symmetry of the two x-intercepts: if roots are r₁ and r₂, the axis is at (r₁ + r₂)/2. Then use Vieta's formulas to show this equals −b/2a — the formula has a reason.

Domain 3 · 18–20% of EOC

Statistics & Probability

Third-highest weight. The new skills relative to Math 8 are standard deviation, regression model selection (linear vs. exponential), and normal distribution intuition.

DATAOne-variable statistics — center and spread together

Retention
NC.M1.S-ID.1–2

Mean and median together: Calculate both for a data set, then add an outlier. Watch the mean move; watch the median barely budge. This is the demonstration that teaches when to use each measure.

Standard deviation intuition: Before computing, ask: "Which data set has more spread: {4, 5, 6} or {1, 5, 9}?" The deviation-from-mean picture comes first. SD is roughly the average distance from the mean.

IQR vs. SD: IQR is resistant to outliers (uses middle 50%); SD is sensitive (uses all values). Pair each with the appropriate center measure: IQR with median, SD with mean.

Box plot ↔ five-number summary: Build the box plot by hand from a data set; then, given a box plot, read back the five-number summary. Both directions on the EOC.

MODELSTwo-variable statistics — choosing the right regression model

Retention
NC.M1.S-ID.6–7

Model selection flow: (1) Make a scatter plot. (2) Does it look linear (constant slope direction), curved upward (exponential?), or U-shaped (quadratic)? (3) Compute residuals — if they pattern (not random), you've got the wrong model. (4) Use the regression line in context.

r and r²: r tells direction and strength of a linear relationship. r² (coefficient of determination) tells the proportion of variation in y explained by x — "84% of the variation in test scores is explained by hours of sleep." The EOC asks for interpretation, not computation.

Residual plot: If residuals show a pattern (curve), the model is wrong. If they scatter randomly around 0, the model fits. Show both; students should be able to diagnose from the plot.

Domain 4 · 8–12% of EOC

Geometry

Lowest-weight domain — allocate time accordingly. The focus is on similarity, congruence, and coordinate geometry connections that reinforce the algebra and functions content.

CONNECTSlope as a similarity argument

Retention
NC.M1.G-GPE

Why is slope constant along a line? Because every right triangle drawn from any two points on the line is similar to every other — corresponding sides are proportional. The rise/run ratio is the same because the triangles are similar. This connects the Geometry and Functions domains meaningfully and gives students a non-procedural explanation for a concept that confuses them.

Coordinate geometry: Midpoint and distance formulas are Pythagorean theorem applications. The midpoint is the average of the two endpoints. Students who remember why they work will reconstruct the formula under pressure.
