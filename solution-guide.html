Solution Formatting Guide

How to write up your work so it earns full credit on EOG and EOC tests — and so any reader can follow your reasoning. Rules for students plus annotated examples and sentence frames for teachers.

For Students — Write It So a Stranger Can Follow It

Scorers on the NC EOG and EOC are strangers. They read hundreds of papers and cannot give you the benefit of the doubt for steps they can't see. Your job is to make your thinking completely visible — even when the answer seems obvious to you.

1

Show every step — even the "obvious" ones

Never jump from the problem to the answer. Write each step on its own line. If you did arithmetic in your head, write it anyway. Scorers award partial credit for each step shown — a wrong final answer with correct steps still earns points.

2

Label what you are finding at each step

Use words, not just numbers. Instead of writing "= 7", write "x = 7" or "slope = 7" or "volume = 7 cm³". The reader should never have to guess what your expression represents.

3

Box or circle your final answer

Scorers skim fast. Circle or box your final answer so it's unmistakably clear where to look. If you have multiple answers (e.g., two solutions to a quadratic), box them both.

4

Include units for context problems

Any problem with real-world context (speed, area, money, temperature) requires units on the final answer. "The car travels at 60" earns less than "The car travels at 60 mph." Missing units is one of the most common unnecessary point losses on EOG.

5

Define your variables

For any word problem, start with a "let" statement: "Let x = the number of small bags" before you use x. This earns points and forces you to think clearly about what you're solving for.

6

Check: does the answer make sense?

After solving, ask: is this reasonable? Can a number of students be negative? Can a length be a decimal? Catching an unreasonable answer and fixing it earns back points. Write a one-sentence reasonableness check at the end of multi-step problems.

7

For "justify" or "explain" prompts: Claim → Evidence → Reasoning

Write a complete sentence with three parts: (1) your claim, (2) the mathematical evidence (a number, equation, or calculation), and (3) one sentence explaining what that evidence means. Never just write a number as your "explanation."

Side-by-Side Examples

Example 1 — Solving a System of Equations

Poor — Likely Loses Points
y = 2x + 1, y = -x + 7
3x = 6
x = 2
y = 5
Missing: where 3x = 6 came from, what (2, 5) represents, no check
Strong — Earns Full Credit
Let x = ?, y = ?
Equation 1: y = 2x + 1
Equation 2: y = −x + 7 [given]
Set equal: 2x + 1 = −x + 7 [substitution]
3x = 6, so x = 2 [add x, subtract 1]
y = 2(2) + 1 = 5 [back-substitute]
Solution: (2, 5)
Check: 5 = −2+7 ✓ [verify in Eq. 2]

Example 2 — "Explain" or "Justify" Prompt

Prompt: "Is the equation 3x + 6 = 3(x + 2) an identity, a contradiction, or a conditional equation? Explain."

Poor — Earns Minimal Credit
Identity.
No work shown, no explanation — just a label. Earns 0–1 point on a 3-point item.
Strong — Earns Full Credit
Distribute: 3x + 6 = 3x + 6 [3·x + 3·2]
Subtract 3x: 6 = 6 — always true [both sides equal]
Identity — the equation is true for all values of x because both sides simplify to the same expression. No matter what x is, the equation holds.

Example 3 — Volume with Context

Prompt: "A soup can has radius 4 cm and height 12 cm. Find its volume. Round to the nearest tenth."

Poor — Loses Points
π(4²)(12)
= 603.2
Missing: formula label, units on answer, no reasoning visible for scorer
Strong — Earns Full Credit
Formula: V = πr²h [cylinder]
V = π(4)²(12) = 192π
V ≈ 603.2 cm³ [rounded to nearest tenth]
Check: reasonable — a can about 8 cm across and 12 cm tall, ~600 cm³ ≈ ⅔ liter. ✓
EOG & EOC — Specific Guidance

Most Math 8 EOG and Math 1 EOC items are multiple-choice or technology-enhanced — but constructed-response items and multi-part items require you to show your work and justify. These items are often worth 2–4 points, and partial credit is available for every step.

⚠️

Multi-step items: Scorers award partial credit for each step. If you don't know the final answer, show all the steps you do know. A partially correct solution with clear work earns more than a blank page or an answer with no work.

✏️

"Explain" prompts: Write at minimum one complete sentence using math vocabulary. Starting with "Because…" or "This means that…" forces you to make a full claim. Vague answers ("it works" or "they are the same") earn zero.

📐

Geometry and context problems: Always include units. "The area is 36" earns less than "The area is 36 square feet." On EOG, one missing unit label can cost a point on a 2-point item.

🔢

Rounding: If the problem does not specify rounding, leave in exact form (like 4π or √50). If it says "nearest tenth," round at the last step — not earlier, to avoid rounding error accumulation.

📋

Showing your formula: For EOG geometry items, always write the formula before substituting values. A formula line (V = πr²h) earns a point even if the calculation contains an error.

💯

Don't leave blank: There is no penalty for guessing on NC EOG/EOC. If you are unsure, eliminate what you can, show whatever reasoning you have, and write your best answer. Partial credit beats zero.

For Teachers — Sentence Frame Bank

These sentence frames scaffold the "justify" and "explain" prompts that appear on EOG and EOC. Post them in the room, include them on assessments, or use them as a starting structure during the Discuss & Lock-In phase. Teaching students to argue mathematically — not just compute — is what moves them from procedural to proficiency.

Making a Claim

Identifying a function family"This is a _____ function because the _____ is constant / ratio is constant / second differences are constant."
Classifying a solution type"The equation has _____ solution(s) because when I simplify both sides, I get _____, which is always true / never true / true only when x = ___."
Right triangle claim"The triangle _____ a right triangle because a² + b² _____ c², specifically ___ + ___ _____ ___."
Slope interpretation"The slope of ___ means that for every one-unit increase in _____, the _____ increases/decreases by ___."

Interpreting Context

Y-intercept in context"The y-intercept of ___ means that when _____ is zero, the _____ is ___."
Exponential model"The initial value is ___. Each _____, the quantity _____ by a factor of ___, which is a ___% growth/decay rate."
Correlation in context"There is a _____ (weak/strong, positive/negative) association between _____ and _____. This means that as _____ increases, _____ tends to _____."
r² interpretation"The r² value of ___ means that ___% of the variation in _____ is explained by its linear relationship with _____."

Comparing & Contrasting Functions

Comparing rates of change"Function ___ grows faster because its slope/rate of change is ___, while function ___'s is ___."
Comparing y-intercepts"Function ___ has a higher starting value because its y-intercept is ___ versus ___'s y-intercept of ___."
Eventually greater"Even though _____ starts higher, _____ is eventually greater because _____ grows exponentially while _____ grows linearly."

Proving & Verifying

Checking a solution"To verify, I substituted x = ___ into the original equation: ___. Both sides equal ___, so x = ___ is correct."
Disproving a claim"This claim is incorrect. A counterexample is x = ___, which gives ___, not ___."
Reasonableness"This answer is reasonable because _____ [connect to context: units, magnitude, sign]."
Common Point-Loss Checklist

Run through this list before submitting any constructed-response or multi-step item:

□

Every step is written out explicitly

□

Variables are defined (let x = …)

□

Final answer is labeled and circled

□

Units are included (cm, mph, $, etc.)

□

Formulas written before substituting

□

"Explain" answer is a complete sentence

□

Rounding done at the final step only

□

Answer checked for reasonableness
