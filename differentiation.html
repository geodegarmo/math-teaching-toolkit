Differentiation Toolkit

Mixed-ability is the default reality in both courses. The goal isn't writing three separate lessons every night — it's building one task with multiple entry points and multiple ceilings. These six moves do that.

The core principle: Differentiation is not about different tasks for different students — it's about one task that different students can enter at different depths. When everyone is in the same problem, discussion is possible. When groups have different problems, the room splits.
Move 1 — The Foundation

Low-floor / high-ceiling tasks

Differentiation

The cornerstone move. Pick tasks anyone can begin but that keep going far past the "right answer." Everyone is in the same problem, working at their own edge — no one is sidelined with a worksheet that's too easy or too hard.

What makes a floor "low": The problem starts with concrete, visual, or familiar context. No abstract notation on first contact. Starter questions anyone can answer ("What do you notice? What do you wonder?").

What makes a ceiling "high": There are always more questions to ask. Generalizing, justifying, finding edge cases, connecting to other representations.

Math 8 example: Show a right triangle on a grid. "How long is the hypotenuse?" — anyone can estimate, most can count grid squares, some will derive Pythagorean theorem. The same prompt reaches every student.

Math 1 example: "A function doubles every step. Build a table, graph, and equation." — low floor (anyone can start doubling), high ceiling (domain/range, long-run behavior, comparing to a linear function that starts higher).

Move 2

Tiered / parallel tasks

Differentiation

Same concept, two or three versions at different complexity, all discussable together. Students (or you) choose the lane. The whole-class discussion still works because everyone explored the same idea.

How to tier: Start with the core task. The scaffold version adds: given information, fewer steps required, a worked first example, or visual supports. The extension version adds: justification required, generalization, or removed scaffolds from the core.

Tier A (scaffold): Solve the system y = 2x + 1 and y = −x + 7. [Equation given in slope-intercept form; graphing grid provided.]

Tier B (core): Solve the system 2x − y = −1 and x + y = 7. [Students choose method.]

Tier C (extension): Create a system that has exactly one solution at (2, 5). How many different systems can you build? What constraints must they satisfy?

Discussion bridge: All tiers can answer "What did the solution mean?" — that question reunites the class regardless of which tier they worked.

Move 3

"Must-do / May-do" structure

DifferentiationPacing

A required core plus a stack of optional extensions. Solves the early-finisher problem and the pacing problem at once — nobody sits idle, you're never scrambling for an extra task, and fast finishers aren't rewarded with more of the same problems.

Must-do: Problems 1–4 (core skill at grade level). Every student is expected to complete these before the exit ticket.

May-do stack (posted on board or in a folder):
· Extension problem pushing the concept further
· Error analysis (find and fix the mistake)
· Explain it in writing (justify your work to a student who was absent)
· Connect to a prior topic (how does this relate to slope?)
· Create your own problem of this type

Why it works for pacing: You stop the class for discussion when most students have finished the must-do, not when all students have — the may-do stack keeps early finishers productive while you wait for the class to converge.

Move 4

Strategic, flexible grouping

Differentiation

Group by today's exit-ticket data, not a fixed "ability track." Sometimes mixed (peers teach each other), sometimes by readiness (you pull a reteach table). The composition changes weekly — that flexibility is what keeps it equitable and avoids the stigma of permanent groups.

Data-driven grouping process: Sort yesterday's exit tickets into got it / almost / not yet. During today's Explore, students who are "almost" or "not yet" sit together for a 5-minute targeted reteach while you circulate. "Got it" students work on the extension lane.

Mixed-ability grouping: Use this when the discussion itself is the learning — when hearing a peer explain their approach is valuable. Avoid it when students need direct instruction on a concept they're missing; mixed groups can produce frustration rather than learning.

Partners vs. groups of 4: Partners for routine practice (faster, more talk time per student). Groups of 4 for rich tasks where multiple approaches are worth comparing. Groups larger than 4 usually have passengers.

Move 5

Sentence frames & explanation scaffolds

Differentiation

Students who understand the math but struggle to articulate it still need to access the discussion. Frames give them the language; they provide the math. This directly rehearses EOG/EOC explanation language.

General frames:
"I knew it was ___ because ___."
"I disagree with ___ because ___."
"Another way to think about this is ___."
"This is similar to ___ because both ___."

Math 8 function frames:
"This is / is not a function because ___."
"The slope means that for every ___, the ___ changes by ___."
"The y-intercept means that when ___ is zero, ___ is ___."

Math 1 function frames:
"I know it's exponential because the ratio between consecutive y-values is ___."
"The vertex tells us that the maximum/minimum value is ___ and it occurs when ___."
"f(3) = 12 means that when the input is ___, the output is ___."

Caution: Frames are scaffolds, not scripts. Retire them as fluency grows — the goal is internalized language, not permanent dependence on the frame.

Move 6

Manipulatives & technology as access ramps

Differentiation

Algebra tiles, Desmos, physical models aren't just for "lower" students — they give everyone a concrete handhold and let struggling students participate in the same rich task rather than a watered-down one.

Algebra tiles: Model the area (x + 3)(x + 2) physically before writing x² + 5x + 6. Students who can build the area model own FOIL; students who memorized FOIL but not the model will confuse themselves under pressure.

Desmos as a differentiation tool: Low floor — everyone can graph. High ceiling — predict before you drag, justify what you see, build your own function with constraints. The same tool scales across ability levels because the tool itself doesn't have a ceiling.

Graph paper: Never underestimate it. A student who draws a table and plots points as a first step to understanding a function is doing legitimate mathematics. The graph is the concrete representation; the equation is the abstract one.

Caution on technology: Use it to build meaning, not replace it. A student who graphs by clicking "plot" without understanding slope will be lost when the calculator isn't available on the paper section of the test.

Open-Middle Problems

Bonus: Open-middle problems for the top end

Differentiation

For mixed-ability classes, swap some "one right answer" problems for tasks with one answer but many paths, or many answers under a constraint. Keeps early finishers genuinely challenged instead of just doing more of the same.

Math 8 example: "Using each digit 1–9 at most once, build a system of two linear equations whose solution is (3, 4)." Trivial to start, hard to optimize.

Math 1 example: "Write an exponential function that passes through (0, 8) and (2, 32). How many functions satisfy this? Prove it." Students who find one solution are challenged to find all of them and justify.

Source: openmiddle.com — searchable by standard and grade level. Most problems are designed for exactly this use case.
