The Daily Structure

The Sticky Math Block

One predictable structure for every period. Students learn the rhythm; the rhythm carries cognitive load. Adopt this as your house style from day one.

Spiral Do-Now 5–8 MIN

Four problems on the board as students arrive: one from yesterday, one from last week, two from earlier high-weight domains (integer operations, slope, exponent rules). Never just today's topic. Domain 1 · 30% of EOG

Number, Expressions & Equations

The highest-weight domain. The traps are rules taught before meaning — students who have memorized exponent rules without deriving them, or who can "do" the procedure but can't explain why.

CRAExponent Rules — derive, don't state

Retention

Never announce the rule. Let students discover it by completing a pattern table first.

NC.8.EE.1

Discover the zero and negative rules: Build the table: 2⁴ = 16, 2³ = 8, 2² = 4, 2¹ = 2 … "What happens each step? So 2⁰ must be ___? And 2⁻¹?" Students who discover 2⁰ = 1 own that fact; students who were told it forget it.

Product rule: Expand x³ · x⁵ = (x·x·x)(x·x·x·x·x) — count the factors. The rule xᵐ·xⁿ = xᵐ⁺ⁿ should emerge from the count, not be announced first.

Power rule: Write out (x³)² = x³·x³ and count. Then ask: what shortcut describes what you're doing?

Quotient rule: Write x⁶/x² as a fraction of expanded factors and cancel. The rule xᵐ/xⁿ = xᵐ⁻ⁿ is now just a description of what cancellation does.

CRAScientific Notation — anchor to magnitude

Retention

Students lose points because they don't develop intuition for whether an answer is reasonable. Fix this with magnitude comparisons first.

NC.8.EE.3–4

Concrete: Place common things on a number line by power of 10 — a bacterium (~10⁻⁶ m), a human (~1 m), the distance to the moon (~4 × 10⁸ m). This builds "size sense" before computation.

Common error: 42 × 10³ ← not in proper form. Have students fix malformed notation before writing their own — error analysis before production.

Estimation gate: Before computing (3.2 × 10⁴)(5.0 × 10²), ask: "The answer will be in the 10___ range." Students estimate first, then compute. This catches decimal-placement errors before they become habits.

PATTERNLinear Equations — the special cases matter most

RetentionDifferentiation

Students routinely solve equations with one solution but crash on "no solution" and "infinitely many." These are high-EOG-frequency items.

NC.8.EE.7

Visual entry: Graph y = 3x + 6 and y = 3(x + 2). Same line — infinitely many solutions. Graph y = 2x + 1 and y = 2x + 5. Parallel — no solution. The algebra mirrors what they see.

Sort activity: Give cards with 12 equations; students sort into three piles: one solution / no solution / infinitely many. Justification card: "I know because when I simplified, I got ___."

Scaffold lane: Provide the first two simplification steps; students complete and classify.
Extension lane: Write an equation in each category and explain how you guaranteed it would land there.

VISUALSystems of Equations — graph before algebra

RetentionDifferentiation

Algebraic case-counting (one / none / infinite) is invisible until students see it first on a graph.

NC.8.EE.8

Sequence: (1) Graph two non-parallel lines → find intersection visually. (2) Set equations equal → solve algebraically → verify it matches the graph. (3) Now introduce substitution as a tool for non-integer intersections.

Real-world hook: Two phone plans — Plan A: $30/mo + $0.10/text, Plan B: $20/mo + $0.20/text. When does Plan A become cheaper? Students graph, find the crossover, then verify algebraically.

Domain 2 · 25% of EOG

Functions

The EOG tests function concepts heavily — definition, notation, comparing functions, and linear function modeling. The concrete entry point is always a real rate-of-change situation before the formula.

CONCEPTWhat is a function? — the vending machine model

Retention

The formal definition ("each input maps to exactly one output") doesn't stick until it's attached to something physical. Use the vending machine: you press B3, you always get Doritos. If sometimes you got Doritos and sometimes Skittles from B3 — that's not a function.

NC.8.F.1

Test for functions: Given a table, graph, equation, or set of ordered pairs, students ask: "Does any input have two different outputs?" Tables: scan for repeated x-values with different y-values. Graphs: vertical line test — if a vertical line crosses more than once, not a function.

Function notation entry: f(3) = 7 reads "when the input is 3, the output is 7." Translate between notation and table: if f(x) = 2x − 1, make the table, make the graph, write three input-output sentences in words.

CRASlope as rate of change — start with physical steepness

RetentionDifferentiation
NC.8.F.4 · NC.8.EE.5

Concrete: Measure the rise and run of an actual ramp or staircase in the building. Which is steeper? Students rank before computing.

Representational: Mark rise/run on a graphed line with a right triangle. Count squares; compute m = rise/run.

Abstract: m = Δy/Δx. The formula is now a shortcut for the counting they already did — not a mystery to memorize.

Negative slope entry: "Going left to right, are you going up or down?" — a question before a rule.

Scaffold lane: Provide the graphed line with the right triangle drawn; students label and compute.
Extension lane: Given the slope, draw two different lines that have it. Are they the same line? Why not?

COMPAREComparing functions across representations

Retention

The EOG regularly asks students to compare two functions given in different forms (one as a table, one as an equation). This requires the ability to extract slope and y-intercept from any representation.

NC.8.F.2

Give: Function A as a graph, Function B as the table x: 0, 1, 2, 3 | y: 5, 8, 11, 14. "Which has the greater rate of change? Which has the greater y-intercept?" Students must read slope from the graph and compute it from the table, then compare.

Non-linear functions: Show a table of f(x) = x² alongside y = 3x. Ask: "For small x, which is larger? For large x?" This builds qualitative function reasoning.

Domain 3 · 25% of EOG

Geometry

Two sub-threads: transformations (congruence and similarity) and measurement (Pythagorean theorem and volume). Both reward a concrete-first approach — cut and fold before writing coordinates.

CRAPythagorean Theorem — why areas, not sides

Retention
NC.8.G.7–8

Concrete: Each student gets square tiles. Build a right triangle with legs 3 and 4 using tiles on the floor. Now build actual squares on each leg — how many tiles? 9 + 16 = 25. Build the square on the hypotenuse. Same 25 tiles. This is why it's the areas, not the sides.

Representational: Draw the squares on grid paper and compute areas. Generalize: any right triangle?

Abstract: Only now: a² + b² = c².

Common error card: Show the work where a student added the sides (3 + 4 = c → c = 7). "What did they think the theorem says? How would you correct them using the tile model?"

Distance formula connection: Plot (1, 2) and (5, 5). Draw the right triangle formed by moving horizontally then vertically. The hypotenuse is the distance — students find it using a² + b² = c².

SEQUENCETransformations — build intuition with tracing paper

DifferentiationRetention
NC.8.G.1–5

Start with tracing paper: Trace a shape, then physically slide (translate), flip (reflect), or turn (rotate) the tracing paper before writing coordinates. The physical action encodes the transformation type.

Congruence vs. similarity: After any rigid transformation (translate, reflect, rotate), angle measures and side lengths are preserved — shapes are congruent. After a dilation, angles are preserved but sides scale — shapes are similar, not congruent.

Coordinate rules to derive:
Reflect over x-axis: (x, y) → (x, −y) — y changes sign.
Reflect over y-axis: (x, y) → (−x, y) — x changes sign.
Rotate 90° CCW: (x, y) → (−y, x) — let students derive this with specific examples before memorizing.

Dilation entry: "If I photocopy a shape at 150%, what changes? What stays the same?" Angles stay, lengths multiply by 1.5. Scale factor = ratio of image length to original length.

FORMULAVolume of Cones, Cylinders & Spheres

Retention
NC.8.G.9

Cylinder–cone relationship: Fill a cone with sand, pour into the matching cylinder (same base, same height). Three cones fill the cylinder exactly. V_cone = ⅓ V_cylinder = ⅓πr²h. Students verify this experimentally before using the formula.

Sphere formula connection: V = ⁴⁄₃πr³. The connection: a sphere of radius r fits exactly inside a cylinder of radius r and height 2r. The sphere's volume is exactly ²⁄₃ of that cylinder's volume — a beautiful relationship worth noting.

Common errors: (1) Using diameter instead of radius — always confirm which is given. (2) Computing πr²h + πr²h instead of multiplying once. (3) Forgetting to cube the radius for spheres when the formula is on the reference sheet.

Domain 4 · 20% of EOG

Statistics & Probability

The lowest-weight domain — don't over-invest weeks here. The key skills are reading scatter plots, interpreting lines of best fit, and using two-way tables.

DATAScatter plots — association before line of best fit

Retention
NC.8.SP.1–3

Entry sequence: (1) Collect real data from the class (shoe size vs. height, hours of sleep vs. quiz score). (2) Students predict association before plotting — "positive? negative? none?" (3) Plot, then discuss: was your prediction right?

Correlation vocabulary: Positive/negative describes direction. Strong/weak describes how tightly points cluster around the trend line. Sketch four scatter plots on the board — strong positive, weak positive, strong negative, no association — students label each with both words.

Line of best fit: Draw by eye first (half the points above, half below, matching the overall direction). Then use the equation: m is the rate of change in context ("for every additional hour of sleep, the quiz score increases by ___"), b is the y-intercept in context.

Interpolation vs. extrapolation: Predicting inside the data range is reliable; predicting far outside is risky. Use a concrete example: predicting height at age 20 vs. predicting height at age 50 using child growth data.

TABLETwo-way tables — relative frequency is the key

Retention
NC.8.SP.4

Real-data entry: Survey your class (e.g., "Do you play a sport? Do you prefer morning or afternoon classes?"). Build the two-way table together on the board.

Joint vs. marginal vs. conditional frequency: Joint = the cell value. Marginal = a row/column total. Conditional = a cell ÷ its row or column total. EOG items ask "of the students who play a sport, what fraction prefer mornings?" — that's conditional.

Association: If conditional frequencies are about the same across rows, there's no association. If they differ, there might be. Walk students through the logic before asking them to conclude from a table.
